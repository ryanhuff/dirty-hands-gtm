# Quotivity — Buyer Personas

---

## Persona 1: VP of Sales / Sales Director

### Role and Seniority

- **Title:** VP of Sales, Sales Director
- **Seniority level:** VP / Director — reports to CRO or CEO
- **Team size:** Manages a growing sales team
- **Scope of responsibility:** Owns quota, revenue targets, and sales team performance. Accountable for deal velocity and rep productivity. Increasingly frustrated by administrative overhead that pulls them away from coaching and strategy.

### Buying Psychology

- **Core motivation:** Removing friction from the sales process so reps spend time selling. Wants fast, accurate quoting that doesn't require hand-holding.
- **Core fear:** Lost deals and slow pipeline because reps are battling broken processes instead of engaging customers. Being unable to see what's being quoted until after deals close.
- **Definition of success:** Quote velocity skyrockets. Quote construction is simplified. Fewer quotes need approval because the data is trusted. Approvals that do happen are strategically relevant, not administrative.
- **Buying trigger:** Realization that sales management is spending more time reviewing quotes than coaching reps. Or a visible revenue miss tied to quoting errors or slow turnaround.

### Pain Points (In Their Words)

1. "Quoting is slow and inaccurate. Reps jump between spreadsheets and outside systems."
2. "Reps are designing quote PDFs themselves. Every quote looks different."
3. "Every quote needs approval because we can't trust the content. I'm buried in administrative review instead of coaching my team."
4. "I can't see what's being quoted until the deal closes."
5. "We're losing deals because it takes too long to get a quote out the door."

### Decision Criteria

1. **Impact on quote velocity** — "Will my reps actually quote faster?"
2. **Rep adoption ease** — "Will my team actually use this, or will they find workarounds?"
3. **Reduction in approval burden** — "Can I stop reviewing every quote and focus on the ones that matter?"
4. **Visibility** — "Can I see what's being quoted in real time?"

### Common Objections (In Their Words)

| What They Say | What They Mean | How to Address It |
|---------------|---------------|-------------------|
| "Does it do [specific feature]?" | They have a specific workflow need and want to make sure it's covered. | Address with education materials. If the feature doesn't exist, discuss roadmap or partner customization in HubSpot. |
| "How is this different from HubSpot's built-in quoting?" | They don't understand why they need something beyond what they already have. | Demonstrate differentiation on complex pricing, product configuration, and policy enforcement. Show what HubSpot CPQ can't do. |
| "My reps won't adopt another tool." | They've been burned by tools that add friction. | Show that Quotivity lives inside HubSpot. Reps don't leave their CRM. It replaces complexity, it doesn't add a new system. |

### Content Preferences

- **Preferred formats:** Short case studies with specific metrics (quote velocity, approval reduction), ROI snapshots, peer testimonials
- **Depth:** Outcome-focused. They want the "what changed" and the numbers, not the technical details.
- **Channels:** LinkedIn, HubSpot community, peer recommendations, industry events
- **Turn-offs:** Technical deep-dives that don't connect to revenue impact. Feature lists without outcomes.

## Persona 2: RevOps Leader

### Role and Seniority

- **Title:** RevOps Manager, Sales Operations Manager, Revenue Operations Lead
- **Seniority level:** Manager / Senior Manager — reports to VP Sales, CRO, or COO
- **Team size:** Often a team of 1-3
- **Scope of responsibility:** Owns the quoting process, templates, policy enforcement, and sales tech stack integration. Responsible for making HubSpot work for the sales team. First to feel the pain of HubSpot CPQ's limitations.

### Buying Psychology

- **Core motivation:** Wants control over templating, policies, and pricing in one place. Wants to build scalable processes instead of patching workarounds.
- **Core fear:** Implementation burden. Adding another tool that they'll have to maintain and troubleshoot. Being responsible for a failed rollout.
- **Definition of success:** Easy management of all CPQ aspects (templating, policies, pricing) without constant maintenance. A quoting process that runs cleanly without daily intervention.
- **Buying trigger:** Hitting the wall with HubSpot's native CPQ. Spending more time patching workarounds than building scalable processes.

### Pain Points (In Their Words)

1. "Quote templates are painful to customize in HubSpot."
2. "Policy enforcement is almost completely lacking. There's no way to prevent bad quotes from going out."
3. "I spend my time patching workarounds instead of building scalable processes."
4. "Every time we add a product or change pricing, I'm updating three different places."

### Decision Criteria

1. **Template management** — "Can I build and manage templates without fighting the system?"
2. **Policy enforcement depth** — "Can I set rules that actually prevent bad quotes?"
3. **Ease of ongoing administration** — "How much of my week will this consume after setup?"
4. **HubSpot integration** — "Does this work with our existing workflows, or does it create a parallel universe?"

### Common Objections (In Their Words)

| What They Say | What They Mean | How to Address It |
|---------------|---------------|-------------------|
| "How big is the implementation?" | They're worried about catalog migration and policy setup landing on their plate. | Be transparent about implementation scope based on catalog size and policy complexity. Show the typical timeline and what support looks like. |
| "Will this break our existing HubSpot workflows?" | They've been burned by tools that conflict with their HubSpot setup. | Demonstrate native HubSpot integration. Show that Quotivity complements existing workflows rather than replacing them. |

### Content Preferences

- **Preferred formats:** How-to guides, implementation walkthroughs, template examples, comparison content (Quotivity vs. HubSpot CPQ capabilities)
- **Depth:** Tactical and specific. They want to see how the product works, not just what it promises.
- **Channels:** HubSpot Community, RevOps-focused Slack communities, LinkedIn, HubSpot Marketplace
- **Turn-offs:** Vague claims about "easy setup." They want specifics about implementation scope and timeline.

## Persona 3: Finance / Controller

### Role and Seniority

- **Title:** Controller, Finance Director, VP Finance
- **Seniority level:** Director / VP — reports to CFO
- **Team size:** Finance team
- **Scope of responsibility:** Cares about margin integrity, discount governance, and reliable reporting on what's being quoted. Often the person manually reviewing every quote to catch discounting issues.

### Buying Psychology

- **Core motivation:** Trust that quotes going out the door are accurate and within policy. Reliable data for reporting and decision-making.
- **Core fear:** Revenue leakage from uncontrolled discounting that they can't see until it hits the P&L. Being the bottleneck on quote approvals because there's no other control mechanism.
- **Definition of success:** Trusting quote integrity. Eliminating the busy work of reviewing every quote just to keep discounting in check. Having reliable reporting on discounting patterns and product combinations.
- **Buying trigger:** Discovery of a significant discounting error or pattern of margin erosion. Or frustration with the volume of quotes requiring manual review.

### Pain Points (In Their Words)

1. "I have no ability to control discounting beyond reviewing every quote that goes out."
2. "I can't reliably report on discounting patterns and product combinations."
3. "I'm doing busy work. Reviewing quotes just to keep discounting under control."
4. "I don't find out about pricing issues until the invoice doesn't match the quote."

### Decision Criteria

1. **Discount controls** — "Can I set limits and escalation rules that actually prevent unauthorized discounting?"
2. **Reporting** — "Can I see discounting patterns and margin impact without building manual reports?"
3. **Quote accuracy** — "Will the quotes that go out be correct without me checking each one?"

### Common Objections (In Their Words)

| What They Say | What They Mean | How to Address It |
|---------------|---------------|-------------------|
| "What does it cost?" | They evaluate everything through an ROI lens. The price needs to be justified by measurable savings. | Frame cost against current revenue leakage. Use the Aptarro (79% reduction in negotiation time) and True North (30% margin recovery) case studies to show concrete ROI. |
| "We already have a process that works." | The current process is painful but familiar. Switching costs feel high. | Acknowledge the process works, then quantify its hidden costs: hours spent on manual review, revenue lost to uncontrolled discounting, delayed deals from slow approvals. |

### Content Preferences

- **Preferred formats:** ROI calculators, case studies with margin and revenue impact metrics, comparison of current-state cost vs. Quotivity cost
- **Depth:** Numbers-focused. They want to see the math.
- **Channels:** Less active on social. More likely to be pulled into the evaluation by the VP of Sales or RevOps leader. Influenced by CFO/finance peer networks.
- **Turn-offs:** Anything that doesn't connect to financial outcomes. Feature lists without dollar impact.
